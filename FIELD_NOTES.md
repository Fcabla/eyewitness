# Your memory is the worst witness: building EYEWITNESS with a 1B model

*Field notes from the Build Small Hackathon — [EYEWITNESS Space](https://huggingface.co/spaces/build-small-hackathon/eyewitness)*

You saw the thief for 3 seconds. Describe him.

Easy, right? You were *there*. You *saw* him. Then the sketch artist draws exactly what
you said — and the face on the paper is a stranger. Welcome to fifty years of eyewitness
psychology research, compressed into a game where the police lineup is built from your
own mistakes.

## The design rule that made a 1B model enough

The whole game runs on one principle: **author the ground truth, don't detect it.**

The engine *composes* every face from a closed vocabulary of attributes (face shape,
hair, brows, glasses, marks…). It never has to recognize anything — it always knows
exactly what the culprit looks like, because it built him. That means:

- difficulty is a dial we control, not a model accident,
- scoring is objective (your words vs. the truth, attribute by attribute),
- and the only job left for a neural network is *translation*: turning
  *"caterpillar eyebrows, looked like he hadn't slept, one of those wool hats"* into
  `{"brows": "bushy", "eyes": "droopy", "hat": "beanie"}`.

That translation job is exactly what a small model is great at. **MiniCPM5-1B** does it
as strict JSON slot-filling over the closed vocabulary, validated with a deterministic
synonym-matcher as backstop — the game literally cannot break on a bad generation. It
handles English and Spanish witnesses in the same breath.

## The signature mechanic: lineups built from your errors

After your testimony, you face the lineup. It isn't random:

- Attributes you got **wrong** get planted on distractors *exactly as you misremembered
  them* — your own sketch misleads you.
- Attributes you **never mentioned** become the confusion axes between near-identical
  suspects.
- Attributes you **nailed** prune the lineup — good memory is rewarded.

We tuned that last rule with a 400-game simulation: in the first version, a witness who
correctly remembered three attributes could uniquely identify the culprit only **2%** of
the time (the lineup cloned everything you knew). One probability change later, the
difficulty curve runs 37% (ROOKIE) → 8% (CHIEF) — winnable, never free. From INSPECTOR
rank, the culprit also *changes one salient feature* between the crime and the lineup.
He's been to a barber since. Of course he has.

## A Gradio 6 war story (so you don't lose the hour we lost)

Switching screens by toggling `gr.Column(visible=...)` works in Gradio 6 — **until a
`demo.load` handler also touches column visibility.** After that, later visibility
updates silently desync client-side (we bisected it with three minimal repros: load-time
updates break it, click-time-only works… once, then a second transition broke too).

The fix that ended the whack-a-mole: rebuild the UI as a **state-driven `@gr.render`
stage**. One `gr.State` dict holds the whole game; every button returns a new state; the
render function draws the current screen. Zero visibility toggling, zero desync. Bonus
gotchas: `gr.HTML` strips `<script>` (our 3-second glimpse timer is pure CSS
`animation-delay`), and `gr.Gallery` treats data-URIs as file paths (rasterize to PIL).

## The Modal factory

Everything expensive runs at build time on Modal, never in the player's path: the crime
case bank is batch-generated by **MiniCPM5-1B-GGUF through llama.cpp**, and the culprit's
verdict lines ("Wrong guy. I walked RIGHT past you. Twice.") are pre-rendered with
**VoxCPM2**. At runtime the game is a ~1B parser plus pure Python — it stays snappy even
when ZeroGPU queues on judging day.

## What playtesting taught us

Humans average around 30% memory accuracy on ROOKIE. Everyone *swears* the hat was a cap.
It was a fedora. The pigeons remember more.
